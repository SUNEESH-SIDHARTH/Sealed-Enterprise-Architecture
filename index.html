What this document is

Sealed Enterprise Architecture (SEA) is a foundational architectural philosophy for building software systems that must outlive their technologies, their teams, their owners, and sometimes even their original purpose. It is not an evolution of any existing methodology. It is a first-principles response to a reality modern enterprises can no longer ignore: the systems we build today must survive changes to everything around them — including the AI tools we build them with.

What SEA Is Not

SEA is not a replacement for any existing architectural framework. It does not compete with TOGAF for enterprise architecture governance, with Domain-Driven Design for domain modelling, with Clean or Hexagonal Architecture for code-level structure, with microservices or event-driven patterns for runtime architecture, or with Scrum or SAFe for team process. SEA addresses one question they have historically left implicit: how does an architectural artefact survive change to the people, technologies, owners, and purposes around it across decades? SEA is designed to sit alongside whichever frameworks an organisation already uses, adding the longevity-and-governance dimension as an explicit layer rather than displacing anything that already works.

Part I — The Problem

1. The Bridge Problem

Consider a bridge commissioned in 1975. Today, fifty-one years later, the bridge still stands and carries traffic. Almost nothing about its context is what it was — the concrete has been resurfaced three times, the original chief engineer retired in 1998, ownership has changed three times, and the towns it connects have transformed entirely.

The reason it works is that civil engineering has developed, over centuries, an implicit discipline for building structures that outlive everything about them. Pillars are sealed — they cannot be modified once poured. New loads are met by adding new pillars alongside, not retrofitting old ones. Road surfaces can change without affecting the span. Ownership transitions come with formal inspection regimes. When a bridge's purpose changes materially, it goes through formal reassessment.

Software engineering has no such discipline.

The tone calibration in 1.1 acknowledges that information hiding (Parnas), bounded contexts (Evans), ports and adapters (Cockburn), ADRs, and semantic versioning each contribute pieces of a long-lived-systems discipline. What is missing is the consolidation — a unified framework that addresses all four change dimensions simultaneously. That is what SEA attempts.

3. Why AI Makes These Gaps Lethal

AI assistants accelerate development by orders of magnitude. They also introduce new failure modes: hallucinated interfaces, silent specification drift, cross-session context loss, model capability regression, and the silent decay of institutional memory across rapidly rotating AI generations. Pre-AI architectures assumed humans held the system in their heads. Post-AI, that assumption is no longer sustainable.

The specific mechanism is velocity amplification: a gap that produced one problematic change per quarter in a human-paced environment produces ten or fifty in an AI-assisted environment. The qualitative problem is the same; the rate of accumulation is catastrophically different.

4. The Failure Mode Catalogue

When the gaps go unaddressed, systems fail in specific, recognisable ways. SEA names fourteen such patterns. Together they form a diagnostic vocabulary with three practical uses: as a diagnostic (walk through each, ask whether your system shows the early signal), as an early warning vocabulary (once named, engineers say "that sounds like Ossification risk" and intervene early), and as a selling tool (concrete failure language lands harder than abstract "technical debt").

FM-01 OssificationThe system is too rigid to change safely. Every modification requires heroic effort. Engineers stop proposing improvements because they know the cost. Early signal: even simple changes require architectural-level discussion.
FM-02 DissolutionThe system loses coherent identity. It does too many things, is owned by too many people, and has no clear seam along which it could be decomposed. Early signal: nobody can describe what the system does in one sentence.
FM-03 Ghost OwnershipA component has a nominal owner who has no actual authority, context, or investment in it. The real owner is unknown or absent. Early signal: a question about a component's behaviour cannot be answered without a multi-team investigation.
FM-04 Purpose DriftThe system now serves a purpose that nobody intended or would have chosen. It serves a constituency of one — the team that built it, whose interests have long since diverged from the organisation's. Early signal: users of the system have stopped requesting improvements.
FM-05 Architectural AmnesiaThe reasons for the system's design have been lost. Engineers maintain the code but cannot explain its shape. Changes that look safe in isolation are unexpectedly catastrophic. Early signal: "I don't know why this is here but I'm afraid to remove it."
FM-06 Vendor CaptureThe system is architecturally dependent on a specific vendor in ways that were not deliberate. Dependencies accumulated until the system cannot be moved without rebuilding substantial portions. Early signal: cost to migrate off vendor X is measured in years, not months.
FM-07 Cognitive DebtNo living human understands the whole system. Knowledge is distributed but not captured. When senior engineers leave, critical integrative knowledge leaves with them. Early signal: architectural questions can only be answered by convening multiple senior engineers.
FM-08 Inheritance FailureThe next generation of engineers cannot maintain what the previous generation built. Tools obsolete, patterns unfamiliar, rationale lost. Rather than maintain, they propose a rewrite — which will face the same fate in a decade. Early signal: "rewrite from scratch" is being seriously considered.
FM-09 Silent Contract ViolationUpstream systems changed in ways that technically violate implicit contracts, but violations are tolerated because the system mostly works. When a new consumer arrives expecting the original contract, everything breaks in hard-to-debug ways. Early signal: the system requires "special knowledge" to integrate with.
FM-10 Compliance RotThe system was compliant when built. Regulations have changed. It is nominally compliant only because the audit process is itself outdated. When a stricter audit arrives, gaps become catastrophically visible. Early signal: last serious compliance audit was more than two years ago.
FM-11 Shadow ArchitectureThe official architecture diagram no longer reflects how the system actually works. Real integrations happen outside the documented boundaries. When engineers who built the workarounds leave, the shadow architecture becomes invisible. Early signal: production incidents trace to components not on any architecture diagram.
FM-12 Model-Induced DriftAI-assisted code from one model generation carries patterns specific to that generation. The next model produces subtly different patterns. Over time the codebase accumulates archaeological strata from different AI generations — internally consistent, not coherent across layers. Early signal: files written a year apart exhibit noticeably different stylistic conventions nobody can explain.
FM-13 Plausibility CascadeCode was added because it looked right. More code was built on top. The original addition turns out to have been subtly wrong — a hallucinated API, an incorrect assumption. But it is now load-bearing. Fixing it requires rebuilding substantial portions. Early signal: a bug investigation turns up something that "should never have worked."
FM-14 Decommissioning ParalysisA system marked for retirement cannot be shut off. A batch process nobody inventoried, a data export nobody documented, a reporting dashboard built years ago — each time decommissioning is attempted, something breaks. Early signal: "I thought we turned that off years ago" is said about a currently-running system.

5. The Stakes

What does it cost to keep building the way we currently build?

Visible costs

  • Rewrite cost — enterprise systems at Inheritance Failure stage typically cost 2-5× their original development budget to rewrite
  • Migration cost — vendor capture and infrastructure lock-in produce migration projects measured in tens of millions for mid-sized enterprises
  • Compliance penalty — regulatory fines for systems past compliance range from hundreds of thousands to billions depending on jurisdiction
  • Incident cost — major production incidents from shadow architecture or plausibility cascades regularly cost seven or eight figures

Invisible costs (larger)

  • Velocity loss — an ossifying system slows feature delivery. A system delivering weekly in year one delivering quarterly in year five has lost most of its economic value without anyone noticing.
  • Opportunity cost — features never even proposed because the architecture was known to make them too expensive. This is the largest invisible cost and nobody tracks it.
  • Talent attrition — engineers leave painful systems. The best leave first. Attrition is rarely attributed to architectural fragility but often stems from it.
  • Strategic inflexibility — an organisation whose core system cannot accommodate a pivot cannot pivot. Market opportunities are missed not because of poor strategy but because architecture forecloses them. This cost is unbounded.
The adoption calculus

A system built with SEA principles costs perhaps 15-25% more to build initially. Over a three-year lifetime, this investment does not pay back. Over a fifteen-year lifetime, the calculation reverses. The compounding value of not-rewriting, not-migrating-under-duress, not-losing-institutional-memory, and retaining strategic flexibility exceeds the upfront cost many times over. SEA is expensive to adopt for short-lived systems and indispensable for long-lived ones.

Part II — The Philosophy

6. The Core Insight

Every architectural failure catalogued in Section 4 has the same underlying cause: the failure to distinguish, explicitly and formally, between what must persist and what must change.

Civil engineering succeeds at long-lived structures because it distinguishes, at every level, which elements are designed to outlast the whole and which are designed to be replaced. Foundations are permanent. Pillars are sealed. Road surfaces are replaceable. Signage is essentially disposable. Each element has a different expected lifetime, a different replacement mechanism, and a different governance model. This is not implicit — it is explicit in the engineering specification, the maintenance manual, the inspection regime, and the budget.

Software engineering rarely makes these distinctions. A codebase is structurally flat. Every line of code has the same status as every other line. There is no formal declaration of 'this is permanent, this is seasonal, this is experimental.'

The core insight of Sealed Enterprise Architecture

The distinction between durable and transient must be explicit, formal, and enforceable.

The Two Layers

The Durable Layer — contracts, interfaces, invariants, intent, data models, and protocols that must persist across all four dimensions of change. These are sealed. They cannot be modified once committed. In a well-designed SEA system, perhaps 10-20% of the total codebase is durable. This is the equivalent of foundations and pillars in a bridge.

The Transient Layer — implementations, optimisations, UI treatments, specific algorithms, infrastructure integrations, and business logic that implements but does not define the system's identity. These are designed to be replaced. Engineers modify them freely. AI assistants can rewrite them at full velocity without endangering the system. The transient layer is most of the codebase.

The Seal Test

"If this element changes, does the system become a different system?"
Yes → durable (sealed). No → transient. If the question cannot be answered cleanly, the element is a candidate for decomposition until the parts can each be answered cleanly.

Applied to a banking system: the definition of an Account is durable. The specific query that returns recent transactions is transient. The invariant that a transaction must be atomic is durable. The specific transaction isolation level is transient.

This distinction, made explicit and enforced, is what makes long life possible. When technology changes, only the transient layer is affected. When teams change, the durable layer serves as the artefact the new team inherits. When ownership changes, the durable contracts are the portable portion that survives the transition. Teams that attempt to implement the mechanism without internalising the distinction produce sealed-looking systems that are sealed in the wrong places — often locking down the transient while leaving the durable exposed.

7. The Seven Principles of SEA

These are axioms. All seven must hold for a system to be SEA-governed. Partial adoption produces partial protection — the gaps in coverage become the points of failure.

P1
Contracts durable, implementations transient
A contract is a separate artefact from the code that implements it. It lives in its own file, under its own governance, expressed in a form that does not depend on the implementation's language or runtime. A Java interface is not a SEA contract. A YAML specification that the Java interface implements is.
P2
Intent captured, not just behaviour
The intent document describes why a contract exists — invariants that must never be violated, alternatives considered and rejected, failure modes the design protects against. Most software decays not because code is lost but because the reasons for the code are lost. SEA makes intent-capture a first-class practice, not an optional documentation task.
P3
Succession is designed, not assumed
Every sealed contract has a formally designated Owner. When the Owner leaves, the succession is formal — a named replacement, a handover ceremony, a shadow period. Most organisations currently assume succession happens naturally. It does not. When lead architects leave, their implicit authority dissipates and the next de facto authority-holder emerges from political dynamics rather than design.
P4
Change is graduated, not binary
Changes move through explicit stages: proposed → reviewed → experimentally implemented → parallel-live → migrated → adopted or formally retired. This principle forbids "just merge it and see what happens." It also provides for safe experimentation: a new approach can be tried alongside the existing one and either adopted or discarded without rushed commitment or silent abandonment.
P5
Purpose-change has its own protocol
When the purpose of a system changes materially — not a feature addition, but a genuine shift in what the system is for — this triggers a Major Version Bump protocol. Current sealed contracts are marked as candidates for retirement, new contracts are designed to serve the new purpose, both operate in parallel during transition, and consumers migrate at their own cadence. This is how bridges get replaced: not demolished, but built alongside.
P6
Portability is a first-class property
Every durable artefact — contracts, intent documents, decision records, governance protocols — is expressed in a form that can be moved between organisations, repositories, and technology stacks without loss of meaning. YAML and Markdown are preferred. Language-specific artefacts are not durable. When ownership transfers, the durable layer transfers intact. When AI tooling changes, the durable artefacts remain readable by whatever tooling replaces the current set.
P7
AI-readability is structural
Every durable artefact is structured for consumption by AI models, not only by humans. The long-term reader of architectural artefacts will increasingly be an AI assistant helping a human understand the system. When the AI can reliably parse the intent of a sealed contract, it becomes a successor mechanism that scales beyond individual human memory. A new principle for 2026.
The seven principles as a unit

None of the seven principles can be adopted in isolation. Contracts without intent are inscrutable. Intent without succession becomes orphaned. Succession without portability cannot survive organisational change. Portability without AI-readability loses its future-proofing. Architectures that adopt only some of the principles produce systems that look sealed but fail in specific ways.

Part III — The Architecture

9. The Five Architectural Layers

1
Contract Definition
Sealed YAML contracts declaring what must persist. Language-agnostic. Immutable once sealed. In a typical enterprise system, perhaps 10-20% of the codebase is durable. This is the equivalent of foundations and pillars.
Contract Owner
2
Intent Capture
Markdown intent documents paired with each contract. Why, not just what. Append-only — history preserved, never overwritten. Intent documents are the mechanism that prevents Architectural Amnesia across team generations.
Contract Owner
3
Verification
Per-stack verifiers that prove implementations conform to contracts. Thin and replaceable — a Kotlin verifier is different from a Python verifier, each reading the same portable YAML contract. Verifiers are transient; contracts are durable.
Platform Team
4
Governance
Three named roles (Contract Owner, Contract Reviewer, Platform Team) and one named body (Sealed Review Board). Without explicit governance, contracts become orphaned. Only the SRB — collective decision, not individual — has authority to retire or replace a sealed contract.
Sealed Review Board
5
Evolution
Three mechanisms: Extension (add new capability alongside, default), Parallel-Live Replacement (new contract runs alongside old, consumers migrate at their own cadence), and Major Version Bump (rare, ceremonial, for genuine purpose change). Each has its own protocol and approval requirements.
Sealed Review Board
The layers are not modular

Layer 1 (contracts) cannot exist without Layer 2 (intent) — an unmotivated contract has no defensible invariants. Layer 3 (verification) cannot exist without Layer 1 — there is nothing to verify against. Layer 4 (governance) cannot exist without Layers 1, 2, and 3. Layer 5 (evolution) operates across all layers simultaneously. Partial adoption of SEA does not provide partial benefit — removing any one layer breaks the protection of the others.

10. The Sealed Contract Format

The format is YAML — chosen for human readability, machine parseability, and portability across tool chains. JSON is acceptable. Language-native formats (Java annotations, TypeScript interfaces) are not acceptable — they fail Principle 6 (portability).

Canonical Schema

seal_id: string              # Globally unique, stable identifier (e.g. "auth.user_session.v1")
version: integer             # Contract version — increments only on parallel-live replacement
status: enum                 # proposed | sealed | parallel-live | deprecated | retired
sealed_date: ISO-8601
sealed_by: string            # The original Contract Owner
current_owner: string        # The CURRENT Contract Owner (not necessarily the original)
current_reviewer: string     # The CURRENT Contract Reviewer
governance_body: string      # Which Sealed Review Board governs this
intent_document: path        # Relative path to the paired Intent Document

interface:
  operations:
    - name: string
      inputs: [type-list]
      outputs: [type-list]
      preconditions: [string]
      postconditions: [string]

invariants:
  - id: string               # Stable ID (INV-1, INV-2...) for cross-referencing
    statement: string        # Plain-language statement
    rationale: string        # Why this invariant exists

extension_policy:
  permitted: [string]        # What kinds of extension are allowed
  forbidden: [string]        # What kinds are prohibited

dependencies:
  - seal_id: string
    version: integer

change_history:              # Append-only — never overwritten
  - date: ISO-8601
    action: enum             # sealed | extended | marked-parallel-live | deprecated | retired
    by: string
    notes: string

Worked Example — auth.user_session.v1

A complete example contract for a simplified authentication session component. This is the kind of contract an enterprise SaaS platform might seal early in its architectural lifecycle:

seal_id: auth.user_session.v1
version: 1
status: sealed
sealed_date: 2026-04-22
sealed_by: Suneesh Sidharthan
current_owner: Suneesh Sidharthan
current_reviewer: Platform-Architect-Rotation
governance_body: Authentication-SRB
intent_document: ./intent/auth-user-session-v1.md

interface:
  operations:
    - name: create_session
      inputs: [user_id: UserIdentifier, device_fingerprint: DeviceFingerprint]
      outputs: [session_token: OpaqueToken, expires_at: Timestamp]
      preconditions:
        - user_id is verified through an authenticated authority
      postconditions:
        - session_token is cryptographically opaque to the client
        - session_token is associated with exactly one user_id
    - name: revoke_session
      inputs: [session_token: OpaqueToken]
      outputs: [revocation_confirmed: Boolean]
      postconditions:
        - if output is true, token is atomically invalidated everywhere

invariants:
  - id: INV-1
    statement: Session tokens are opaque to clients
    rationale: Exposing user identifier in the token permits lateral account compromise via token inspection.
  - id: INV-2
    statement: Session revocation is atomic — cannot partially fail
    rationale: Partial revocation leaves stale sessions that appear valid to downstream services.
  - id: INV-3
    statement: A session must not outlive the user account it represents
    rationale: Sessions tied to deleted accounts are an error state, not a valid state.

extension_policy:
  permitted:
    - New optional output fields on create_session
    - New capability operations that internally call create_session or revoke_session
    - New invariants that strengthen but do not weaken existing ones
  forbidden:
    - Changes to existing operation signatures
    - Removal or weakening of any invariant
    - Non-opaque session token formats (violates INV-1)

dependencies:
  - seal_id: identity.user.v2
    version: 2

change_history:
  - date: 2026-04-22
    action: sealed
    by: Authentication-SRB
    notes: Initial sealing. Replaces unstructured session handling. See intent document for full context.

The Paired Intent Document

The contract above references an intent document. That document would include: the problem statement (two production incidents PI-2025-04 and PI-2025-11 stemming from inconsistent session semantics), invariant rationale (PI-2025-04 traced to base64-encoded user IDs in tokens enabling lateral account compromise; PI-2025-11 traced to revocation that succeeded in auth service but failed silently in session cache for 40 minutes), rejected alternatives (JWT sessions — rejected because not opaque, violating INV-1; account-coupled session lifecycle — rejected because it prevents account compromise recovery), and assumptions (central session store accessible to all services; token opacity backed by at least 128 bits of cryptographic randomness; revocation propagation time bounded at 100ms).

Format Design Notes

Why YAML and not language-native: A Java interface is tied to Java. A TypeScript interface is tied to TypeScript. A sealed contract must outlive both. YAML is a serialization format with wide tool support, human readability, and no execution semantics binding it to any runtime.

Why intent document is separate prose, not more YAML: Intent is narrative. The reasoning behind an invariant, the alternatives considered, the assumptions made — these are not naturally expressed as structured data. Forcing them into YAML fields would produce either sparse stub-like content or unreadable bloat. Markdown prose is the appropriate format for narrative content.

Why invariants have IDs: Invariants are cross-referenced — from the intent document, from test cases, from decision log entries, from audit records. Stable IDs (INV-1, INV-2) permit this cross-referencing without ambiguity. IDs never change, even if the invariant statement is later clarified.

Why extension policy is explicit: Without an explicit policy, engineers will extend sealed contracts in whatever way seems convenient, and over time the 'sealed' contract will drift. An explicit policy — 'these kinds of extension are permitted; these are not' — provides a rule that can be checked automatically where possible and manually where not. It is the first line of defence against the Dissolution failure mode.

11. The Governance Protocol

Architectures that describe sealed contracts but do not specify how sealing is enforced produce documentation, not architecture. This section specifies the operational protocol that turns documentation into discipline.

Extension Review Protocol

The most common governance activity. When a team needs to extend a sealed contract, the extension goes through this protocol before any code is written: Proposer drafts an extension specification → sends to Contract Owner and Reviewer → Owner checks against intent document → Reviewer checks technical constraints → both approve or one proposes changes → on implementation, change_history is updated and intent document gets a new section. Extension reviews should complete in 3-5 working days. If they consistently take longer, one of three things is wrong: contracts are too coarse, review burden is too concentrated, or the review process itself is too heavy.

Quarterly Contract Review Agenda

Every 90 days, the SRB reviews: Contract Health (confirm all Owners and Reviewers are current and active — vacancies must be filled before the meeting closes), Drift Incidents (classify severity, approve remediation), Pending Extensions, Contract Metrics (change velocity, extension approval rate, age of oldest sealed contract), Retirement Candidates (contracts with zero active consumers), and Policy Adjustments. For a well-governed portfolio, quarterly reviews take 90-120 minutes. Longer reviews indicate accumulated drift.

Succession Transfer Protocol

When a Contract Owner or Reviewer leaves their role: Advance notice (ideally 30+ days) → Successor identified by SRB (default candidate is the current Reviewer) → Shadow period (minimum two weeks, shadowing all Owner activities) → Handover session (contract history, major decisions, open issues, tacit knowledge) → Intent document updated (any tacit knowledge surfaced during handover is captured) → Formal transfer (current_owner field updated, SRB witnesses the change). Informal handovers lose information. The intent document update is the most important part of the protocol — it is how a system's knowledge of itself persists across generations of maintainers.

Major Version Review Protocol

Triggered by: fundamental regulatory change that invalidates the contract's design, business model pivot, technology shift so profound that maintaining current contracts costs more than replacing them, or discovery of a flawed invariant that cannot be strengthened without breaking existing consumers. Steps: formal written proposal → executive co-sponsor required → minimum 60-day review period (current contracts remain sealed and unmodified) → replacement design → SRB unanimous approval (not majority) → parallel-live transition (typically 6-12 months) → migration period → retirement ceremony → version increment. An enterprise system will undergo an MVB approximately every 5-10 years.

The governance trade-off

SEA governance is more formal than most current practice. This is a deliberate trade-off. Looser governance produces faster short-term delivery and faster long-term decay. Tighter governance produces slower short-term delivery and dramatically better long-term resilience. For systems with expected lifetimes over ten years, the trade favours tighter governance.

12.9 Why SEA Does Not Become Bureaucracy

Every governance framework that did not actively design against this risk has, eventually, become bureaucracy. SAFe is the canonical cautionary tale: sound underlying ideas that, in many enterprise deployments, became ceremony, role proliferation, and process overhead. SEA must address this risk explicitly.

Six design choices that prevent governance overload:

The Seal Test bounds what gets sealedMost artefacts in a system are not contracts and should never be sealed. A typical enterprise system has tens of thousands of code elements and perhaps a few dozen sealed contracts. Sealing more is a smell, not a virtue.
Extensions replace rewritesThe default response to a change request is to add a new contract or extend an existing one — not to reopen a sealed contract. Most change activity does not touch the governance machinery at all.
Team-autonomous mode is first-classA single team can adopt SEA without any organisational commitment. The team becomes its own Sealed Review Board. Not a degraded version of SEA — a recognised operating mode with its own validity.
Sealed Review Boards are local where they can beThe right scope is the smallest scope that covers the contracts whose evolution requires coordination — and only that scope. Domain-specific boards are legitimate.
Verification is automated where possibleVerifiers run in CI/CD. Compliance reports are generated by tools, not produced as committee deliverables. When verification becomes a meeting, it becomes ceremony. When it remains a pipeline gate, it remains discipline.
Framework names its own failure modesSEA explicitly names anti-patterns such as "sealing too much," "Sealed Review Board theatre," and "Major Version Bump avoidance." Adopters who notice their SEA practice drifting toward ceremony should return to the minimum viable form — fewer sealed contracts, more extensions, more automation.

13. Closing — What This Unlocks

Five concrete capabilities current practice does not provide

1
Change technology without changing architecture
Migrate from Java to Kotlin, MySQL to PostgreSQL, on-premise to cloud, without disturbing sealed contracts. The durable layer persists through technology transitions that would cause rewrites in current practice.
2
Change teams without losing institutional memory
Intent documents and sealed contracts survive the departure of their authors. New engineers have a coherent story to learn — not an accretion of tribal knowledge that must be absorbed osmotically over years.
3
Change ownership without rebuilding
When systems transfer between companies, the durable layer is the portable portion. Successor owners inherit not just the code but its architectural rationale, in a form consumable without involving the previous organisation.
4
Change purpose without ossification
The Major Version Bump protocol provides a formal, high-ceremony, but bounded mechanism for genuine transformation. Systems can evolve through purpose change (from physical-first to digital, from human-operated to AI-augmented) rather than total rewrite.
5
Survive AI evolution
Future AI models can read the durable layer and work effectively against it. The organisation is not locked to any particular AI vendor, model family, or interaction paradigm. Each of the first four capabilities is AI-enhanced when sealed contracts are written with AI-readability in mind.

What is honestly difficult

  • The upfront investment. Sealing contracts takes time. Writing intent documents takes more time. In the first 12-18 months of adoption, delivery velocity will be slower, not faster. Organisations that cannot tolerate this short-term cost will not successfully adopt SEA.
  • The cultural shift. Engineers accustomed to modifying any code at will must accept that some code is formally off-limits for modification. Organisations with 'move fast and break things' cultures will find SEA adoption harder.
  • The risk of theatre. The biggest risk is adopting the vocabulary without adopting the discipline — sealed contracts produced but not actually protected, governance meetings that do not actually govern, intent documents written once and never updated. Preventing this requires genuine executive commitment to long-term architectural health.
The deeper point

Software engineering will either develop the discipline of long-lived structures, or we will continue to rebuild from scratch every decade — and that cost, amortised across the industry, across sectors, across decades, is incalculable.

Build bridges, not bonfires.

Annex A — MiVA as a Validation Case

One working implementation of a subset of SEA principles exists: the MiVA Education platform, built between October 2025 and April 2026 by a solo engineer across five generations of AI models. This is presented as validation, not origin — SEA is the theory that MiVA, in retrospect, was groping toward. MiVA applied a subset of SEA principles, validated that the subset worked, and provided the empirical basis for articulating the full framework.

SEA PrincipleMiVA implementation
P1 — Contracts durableApplied. A sealed API interface with ~20 functions was defined. Implementations were replaced multiple times without modifying the interface.
P2 — Intent capturedPartially applied. A Single Source of Truth (SSOT) document captured substantial intent, but structured 'rejected alternatives' and 'invariants with rationale' sections were not initially formalised.
P3 — Succession designedNot applied. Solo owner throughout. Succession was not exercised.

What MiVA validated

  • Sealed contracts survive AI model transitions. Across five model generations, the sealed API interface did not need modification. Adaptations happened in the transient implementation layer, not the durable one.
  • Drift is real and traceable. Four specific drift incidents (CD1–CD4) occurred. Each was traceable to a missing SEA principle. Had SEA been applied in full, each would have been prevented.
  • Intent capture is the single most valuable practice. Even the partial intent capture MiVA applied was repeatedly cited as the difference between catching errors and shipping them. This is the empirical basis for treating Layer 2 as foundational, not optional.
  • Cross-family AI review catches what same-family review misses. When ChatGPT reviewed Claude-generated code (and vice versa), it caught failure modes that same-family review systematically missed.
An honest limitation

The empirical foundation of SEA 1.1 remains a single solo build over twelve months. The framework is internally coherent and individually principled, and the failure modes it names have been observed in many other systems. But the principles, taken together as a unified whole, have not yet been validated at enterprise scale, with multiple owners, across decades. Readers evaluating SEA for a serious commitment should treat it as theoretically grounded but empirically incomplete. Subsequent versions will incorporate enterprise adoption experience as it accumulates.

Annex A.6 — Hypothetical Applications

Three thought experiments — explicitly labelled as hypothetical, not case studies. No organisation has yet conducted any of these as described. They illustrate how the framework would apply.

Scenario 1 — Authentication provider replacementA mid-sized enterprise built its application on a third-party identity provider in 2020. Five years later, the provider raises pricing or is acquired and degraded. Under SEA, the durable layer would be a sealed contract specifying the authentication operations the application requires — sign in, sign out, session validation, identity claims, MFA enrolment — without reference to any specific provider. The transient layer would be the adapter to the current provider. Migration becomes a parallel-live exercise: a second adapter is built against the new provider, both run side-by-side under traffic shadowing, and the cutover happens once the new adapter has demonstrated parity. The sealed contract does not change. No application code that consumed authentication had to know the provider had changed.
Scenario 2 — LLM provider migrationAn organisation built an AI-augmented product on Provider A's LLM in 2024. By 2027, costs, latency, regulation, or capability mismatch make Provider B preferable. Migration is notoriously painful because prompts are typically embedded in code and model-specific behaviours are exploited inadvertently. Under SEA, the durable layer would be sealed contracts specifying each LLM-using capability with declared invariants (output format, semantic constraints, hallucination tolerances, latency bounds, cost ceilings). Prompts and model selection become transient implementation details. Migration is a verification exercise: do the new model and prompts satisfy the same sealed contract? The application code does not change; only the adapter does. The organisation gains the option to mix providers per contract.
Scenario 3 — Mergers, acquisitions, and continuityA regulated mid-sized enterprise is acquired. Without SEA, integration typically means a "rediscovery" project of 6-18 months in which engineers attempt to reconstruct rationale from code, deprecated documents, and the memories of departing engineers — often unsuccessfully. Under SEA, the acquired company hands over its sealed contracts, intent documents, change histories, and verification reports — portable, version-controlled, machine-readable artefacts. Successor Owners are formally named via the succession protocol. The SRB absorbs the acquired contracts into its quarterly review cadence. Compliance evidence is already structured for audit. The post-acquisition integration becomes months-long rather than multi-year, because the durable layer survived the ownership change intact. This is, in some sense, the canonical SEA value proposition.

The honest positioning

Enterprise architecture has TOGAF. Domain modelling has DDD. Code architecture has Clean and Hexagonal. Team process has Scrum and SAFe. Service architecture has Microservices patterns. AI-augmented coding is beginning to have Spec-Driven Development. Every one of these is good, well-established, and widely adopted.

But none of them answer two specific questions that matter in 2026:

  1. How does the architecture survive when the AI models authoring it change every six months? Every established framework assumes a stable human author. Stable AI author is not a property of current AI, and won't be for the foreseeable future.
  2. How do the durable decisions survive the next decade of changes in team, ownership, purpose, and technology? Architectural frameworks describe static structure well. Almost none specify how structure evolves across generations of custodians.

SEA 1.1 addresses these questions explicitly. The AI-Native SDLC v2.0 operationalises the answers at team level. Neither framework competes with TOGAF or DDD or Scrum — they complement and extend what those frameworks already do.

Domain-Driven Design
Domain Modelling Approach
✓ What DDD does well

Aggregate roots, bounded contexts, ubiquitous language. A vocabulary for expressing domain structure that survives technology change. Strategic patterns (context mapping) for organisational-scale modelling.

✗ What DDD doesn't address

DDD gives you the shape of good domain boundaries but not the formal mechanism for sealing them. No specification of how aggregate roots are versioned, governed, or succeeded. No built-in protocol for extension vs replacement.

+ How SEA complements

Aggregate roots map directly to sealed contract boundaries. Bounded contexts become governance domains with their own SRBs. SEA's evolution mechanisms (extension, parallel-live, MVB) give DDD-modelled systems the governance they otherwise lack.

Code-Level Architecture
✓ What Clean/Hexagonal does well

Ports and adapters. Dependency inversion. Clean separation between business logic and infrastructure. Code that tolerates technology change because infrastructure is replaceable.

✗ What Clean/Hexagonal doesn't address

Clean tells you how to structure code for replaceability, but not how to formally seal the port definitions. Ports are conventions, not contracts. Nothing prevents casual modification, hash-verifies conformance, or governs evolution.

+ How SEA complements

Ports are SEA sealed contracts. Adapters are transient implementations. The Clean conceptual structure stays identical; SEA adds formal sealing, intent capture, and governance around the ports you already have.

✗ What Scrum/SAFe doesn't address

Microservices gives you services that can evolve independently, but not the governance that ensures their public APIs evolve safely. No formal mechanism for API versioning discipline, consumer tracking, or cross-service invariant enforcement.

Adoption recipe — Scrum team already using DDD

Concretely, how does a mid-sized engineering team already running Scrum with DDD patterns integrate SEA and SDLC v2.0 without replacing anything?

  1. Keep your Scrum cadence. Your sprints, standups, retros, and planning meetings continue unchanged. SDLC v2.0 phases happen within the work already scheduled, not in parallel to it.
  2. Identify your aggregate roots. Your DDD modelling has already surfaced the natural sealing boundaries. Each aggregate root is a candidate for a sealed contract. Pick 3-5 most critical ones for Phase 1.
  3. Adopt team-autonomous mode first. Your team becomes its own Sealed Review Board. Contract Owner and Reviewer are senior members of the team. No enterprise commitment required.
  4. Add the RECONCILE weekly sweep. One hour per week applying PL-08 across the five reconciliation pairs for your sealed contracts. Finds drift within days instead of months.
  5. Use SDLC v2.0's VERIFY with your existing QA process. The five compliance states complement — not replace — your existing test suite. QA catches behavioural defects; VERIFY catches architectural drift.
  6. Escalate to enterprise SEA only when warranted. If the organisation decides to adopt at enterprise level, your Phase 1 work transitions to Phase 2 without rework — you were already producing SEA-compatible artefacts.
The meta-point

If a framework you already use is not in this list, it probably still applies. SEA and SDLC v2.0 are not domain-specific — they work with any existing architectural or process framework that leaves artefact lifecycle as an open question. Which is most of them.
